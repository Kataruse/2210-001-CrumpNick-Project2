Sorting Algorithms Report

   For this project, we were tasked with picking two sorting algorithms, one recursive and one iterative, and were told to implement and test their effectibility with sorting integers and books. For our sorting algorithms, we choose Cocktail Sort for iterative and QuickSort for Recursive.
   Cocktail sort is a known by many names, such as the Shaker Sort, Shuttle Sort, Happy Hour Sort, and Ripple Sort. The Complexity at its best case is O(n), and at it's wose its O(n^2) which is also its average case. It is a variation of Bubble Sort where instead of only going through an array until the end, Cocktail goes up to the end then back to the beginning like shaking a Cocktail. How Cocktail sort works is it 1. starts from the beginning of the array and then compares each adjacent pair of elements to check and see if they are in the wrong order to swap them, then 2. it continues this process until you reach the end of the array, then 3. it moves in the opposite direction from the end of the array to the beginning to compare each adjacent pair of elements and swaps them if necessary, then 4. continues to iterate through the array until the beginning again, then finally 5. repeat steps 1-4 until the array is sorted.
   Quicksort is an algorithm based on Divide-and-Conquering. At its best its Ω(nlog(n)), and at it's worse its O(n^2). How Quicksort works is it 1. selects a pivot element, generally in the middle of the array, then 2. partitions the array around the pivot or splits it in half, moving all ellements left of the pivot to the pivot's left and all elements greater than or equal to the pivot's right, then 3. the pivot should be in the correct position, then 4. repeat steps 1-3 on the left partition recursively, then 5. repeat steps 1-3 on the right partition of the pivot recursively, then finally 6. stop the recursion when it can no longer partition itself anymore, as all values should be sorted.

Procedure

When researching and looking into both we agreed that Quicksort would ultimately sort a lot quicker while Cocktail sort wouldn't really be all that effective, but after looking into the outcomes of our data, it really surprised us.

   Looking into the graphs for Cocktail sort the differences between sorting the Book arrays and Integer arrays are very intresting. For integers, having the data in reverse or random order made it run for a considerable longer amount of time than having them almost in order or in order as since it goes up and down the array to manipulate positions, its only natural these would be the most effective, but when looking into books the results are very must the opposite. When looking at the graph you can see that reverse order actually did the best for Cocktail sort while random order took the longest followed by in order and almost in order. This was most likely due to the extra sorting required for books as its not just one variable we're looking at its publish date, last name, first name, and title as well and I suppose reverse order just makes it so it has less hoops to jump through to sort the dataset in that situation.
   Looking into the graphs for QuickSort you see result that are similar to Cocktail sort in some ways while taking a whole lot less time to run. For integers, having the data in order took the least amount of time, followed by almost in order, then reverse order and lastly random order. These results are likely due to the nature of QuickSort's algorthim as the more in order you are the less amount of recursions you'd need to sort the array. Meanwhile on the book graph you see a kinda similar story with the caveat being that now being in order takes a consideratly more amount of time than before, almost the same as reverse order. Going from least effective to most effective we have the randomly ordered dataset, followed by the in order dataset, then slightly quicker we have the reverse order dataset, then with being almost in order the most effective dataset. I feel like these results are likely caused by the same situation as before as since the almost in order dataset has a lot of less recursions it needs to sort its data, it takes the least amount of time, we have no idea why in order takes as long as it does though. Perhaps its due to the way the hierarchy of sorting works.
